# Root Cause Analysis

<strong style="color: #FFD60A; display: block; margin-top: 16px; margin-bottom: 4px;">WHY FIVE TRANSACTIONAL "NEAR ME" KEYWORDS DROPPED 70-87 POSITIONS SIMULTANEOUSLY</strong></p><p>The simultaneous collapse of five high-intent keywords across different service categories in a single month is unlikely to be coincidental and points to a shared underlying cause rather than individual page issues.<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;"><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Broad algorithmic sensitivity</strong>: "Near me" and "local roofer in my area" queries are heavily influenced by Google Business Profile signals, proximity data, and real-time local authority. A shift in how Google weights these signals — consistent with the spring 2026 emphasis on hyper-local optimization noted in industry research — could have deprioritized pages that lack strong GBP reinforcement.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Competitor content improvements</strong>: Industry research on recovering declining roofing rankings specifically identifies competitor content upgrades as a primary cause of sudden position drops. If local competitors like alankunsmanroofing.com or homeshieldpros.com published new service + location pages or improved their GBP profiles this month, they could have displaced Valley Peak on these high-competition "near me" queries.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Content freshness signals</strong>: The research context notes that "companies publishing outdated 2020-style content are losing leads to agencies leveraging entity authority." If the pages targeting these keywords haven't been updated recently, Google's freshness signals may have downgraded them relative to competitors with more recently refreshed content.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>The cause is not fully determinable from available data</strong>, but the pattern — multiple transactional terms dropping to positions 90–99 in the same month — correlates with either a local algorithm update or a competitive displacement event rather than a technical site issue (which would affect all keywords more uniformly).</li><br></ul><strong>Conclusion: These keywords require immediate page-level audits, GBP signal reinforcement, and content freshness updates before May's spring storm season peaks.</strong></p><p><strong style="color: #FFD60A; display: block; margin-top: 16px; margin-bottom: 4px;">WHY CLICKS DROPPED -30% WHILE IMPRESSIONS GREW +5.7%</strong></p><p>This is the central paradox of April's data and has a clear structural explanation when the position distribution is examined.<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;"><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Position distribution is concentrated in the 11–30 range</strong>: With <strong>71 keywords in positions 11–20</strong> and <strong>65 keywords in positions 21–30</strong>, the majority of the ranked portfolio sits on page two and the bottom of page one — positions where CTR is typically below 1%. These keywords generate impressions but almost no clicks.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>The loss of page-one transactional keywords removed the highest-CTR assets</strong>: Keywords at positions 4, 9, and 11 (the five that dropped) would have carried CTRs of 5–15% at those positions. Their removal from page one eliminated the click-generating core of the portfolio even as lower-position keywords continued accumulating impressions.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>20 problem keywords with high impressions and low clicks</strong>: The data explicitly flags <strong>20 problem keywords</strong> with high impressions but low click rates, confirming that a significant portion of the impression base is structurally non-converting at current positions.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Average position degraded 1.5 positions MoM</strong>: Even a modest average position decline across a large keyword set shifts the impression-weighted average deeper into low-CTR territory.</li><br></ul><strong>Conclusion: The click collapse is a direct consequence of losing page-one transactional rankings while retaining (and growing) a large base of page-two impressions. Recovering the five dropped keywords to page one would disproportionately restore click volume.</strong></p><p><strong style="color: #FFD60A; display: block; margin-top: 16px; margin-bottom: 4px;">WHY LOCAL BIRDSBORO AND HAMBURG KEYWORDS SURGED WHILE "NEAR ME" TERMS COLLAPSED</strong></p><p>The data shows a striking divergence: geo-specific terms like "roofing company birdsboro pa" (position 96.5 → <strong style="color: #34C759;">7.3</strong>) and "siding contractor hamburg pa" (position 86 → <strong style="color: #34C759;">10.5</strong>) made dramatic gains, while generic "near me" terms collapsed simultaneously.<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;"><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Geo-modified vs. proximity-based query types</strong>: Google treats explicit city-name queries differently from "near me" queries. City-name queries rely more heavily on on-page content signals (title tags, H1s, service area page content), while "near me" queries weight GBP proximity and review signals more heavily. Valley Peak's content appears to be better optimized for explicit geo-modifiers than for proximity-based intent signals.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Service area page effectiveness</strong>: The gains in Birdsboro and Hamburg suggest that location-specific content or service area pages for those communities are performing well. The "near me" losses suggest the site's GBP or proximity signals may not be as strong for broader, non-city-specific queries.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>This pattern is consistent with industry research</strong>: The spring 2026 roofing SEO research notes that "near me" queries rely on GBP and citations, while city-name queries respond to on-page optimization. The data pattern aligns precisely with this distinction.</li><br></ul><strong>Conclusion: Valley Peak has strong on-page local content but may have a GBP signal gap for proximity-based queries. Strengthening GBP with weekly posts, geo-tagged photos, and review velocity could close this gap.</strong></p><p><strong style="color: #FFD60A; display: block; margin-top: 16px; margin-bottom: 4px;">WHY KEYWORD CHURN IS SO HIGH (1,445 LOST, 1,242 GAINED)</strong></p><p>The volume of keyword movement — over 2,600 keywords entering or exiting rankings in a single month — is disproportionately large relative to the 347-keyword total portfolio and warrants a structural explanation.<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;"><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Ranking volatility at the margins</strong>: Many of the keywords being gained and lost are likely in the position 50–100 range, where rankings are inherently unstable and small content or authority changes cause frequent entry/exit from tracked positions. This is normal at a domain authority of <strong>21</strong>, where the site lacks the authority buffer to hold marginal rankings against competitive pressure.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Domain Authority of 21 creates fragility</strong>: At DA 21 with <strong>85 referring domains</strong>, Valley Peak lacks the link authority to maintain stable rankings for competitive "near me" terms. Industry research consistently identifies backlink authority as a key factor in ranking stability for roofing keywords.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>The net loss of 203 keywords is the real concern</strong>: Gaining 1,242 new keywords while losing 1,445 suggests the site is expanding into new long-tail territory (likely from new content) while losing grip on previously held terms — a pattern consistent with content expansion without sufficient authority reinforcement.</li><br></ul><strong>Conclusion: The churn rate reflects a domain authority ceiling. Backlink building to push DA above 25–30 would stabilize rankings and reduce monthly volatility.</strong></p><p><strong style="color: #FFD60A; display: block; margin-top: 16px; margin-bottom: 4px;">WHY ENGAGEMENT RATE IMPROVED DESPITE TRAFFIC DECLINES</strong></p><p>Engagement rate rose to <strong style="color: #34C759;">46.6%</strong> (<strong style="color: #34C759;">+4.2pp MoM</strong>) even as organic sessions fell <strong style="color: #FF453A;">-23.7%</strong> and total sessions declined <strong style="color: #FF453A;">-1.1%</strong>.<ul style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 20px;"><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Traffic quality improvement through self-selection</strong>: When lower-intent, high-bounce traffic (often from broad "near me" queries at marginal positions) exits the portfolio, the remaining traffic skews toward higher-intent visitors who engage more deeply. The loss of position-25+ impressions may have paradoxically improved the quality of who actually clicks through.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Average session duration of 141 seconds</strong>: At 2 minutes 21 seconds average, users who do arrive are spending meaningful time on the site — consistent with the engagement rate improvement and suggesting the site content is resonating with the visitors who find it.</li><li style="margin-bottom: 4px;"><strong>Lead volume held at 17</strong>: Despite the traffic declines, <strong style="color: #BF5AF2;">17 legitimate leads</strong> were generated, suggesting conversion efficiency is strong. The traffic that is arriving is converting at a healthy rate relative to session volume.</li><br></ul><strong>Conclusion: The engagement improvement is a genuine quality signal, not a consolation metric. The site converts well when the right traffic arrives — the priority is restoring volume of that right traffic.</strong>
